# Why the Sky Is Blue

Almost everyone has looked up on a clear day and wondered why the sky is blue rather than green, purple, or simply white. The answer is a small lesson in how light and air behave together, and it has surprised curious people for centuries.

## Light Is Not One Color

Sunlight looks white, but it is really a mixture of every color. When sunlight passes through a glass prism, it spreads into a rainbow: red, orange, yellow, green, blue, and violet. Each color is a wave, and the waves are not all the same size. Red light travels in long, lazy waves. Blue and violet light travel in short, quick waves.

This difference in wavelength turns out to be the whole secret.

## The Air Scatters Light

Our atmosphere is full of tiny molecules of gas, far too small to see. When sunlight enters the air, it bumps into these molecules and bounces off in all directions. Scientists call this **scattering**.

Here is the key fact: short waves scatter much more strongly than long waves. Blue light, with its short wavelength, gets thrown around the sky again and again. Red light mostly passes straight through. So when you look up, the blue light is arriving at your eyes from every part of the sky at once, and the sky appears blue.

## Then Why Are Sunsets Red?

At sunset the sun sits low, and its light must travel through far more air to reach you. Along that long path, almost all the blue light is scattered away before it arrives. What remains is the red and orange light, which is why the evening sky glows with warm colors.

The same physics explains two very different sights. At noon the scattered blue dominates; at dusk the surviving red takes over.

## A Surprising Detail

If short waves scatter the most, you might expect the sky to look violet, since violet waves are even shorter than blue ones. Part of the answer is that the sun sends out less violet light to begin with. The rest is about us: human eyes are simply more sensitive to blue than to violet. The sky we see is shaped not only by the air, but by the instrument we use to observe it.

So the next time the sky stretches blue above you, remember that you are watching countless invisible collisions, sorted by the size of waves, and filtered by your own remarkable eyes.
